Colby's Top 3 Favorite Studio Ghibli Movies




3. The Boy and The Heron (2023)

Hayao Miyazaki’s long-awaited return is a haunting, visually stunning meditation on grief, memory, and the thin veil between worlds.

Review Summary

With The Boy and the Heron, Hayao Miyazaki delivers a deeply personal and enigmatic story that feels both familiar and unlike anything he’s done before. Loosely inspired by Genzaburō Yoshino’s 1937 novel How Do You Live?, the film is less a direct adaptation and more a spiritual exploration of themes Miyazaki has grappled with throughout his career—loss, transformation, and the strange beauty of the unknown.

At its center is Mahito, a boy reeling from the death of his mother, whose journey into a dreamlike parallel world becomes a metaphor for grief and growth. The film eschews straightforward narrative in favor of layered, often surreal imagery—giant parakeets, crumbling towers, and cryptic guides like the mysterious heron—each symbolizing inner turmoil and philosophical riddles.

Visually, it’s quintessential Ghibli: hand-drawn animation of breathtaking detail and quiet wonder, with compositions that linger and landscapes that feel alive. But this isn’t a crowd-pleasing fantasy in the vein of Spirited Away or My Neighbor Totoro. It’s slower, more introspective, and at times intentionally opaque, a film that invites interpretation rather than offering resolution.

Ultimately, The Boy and the Heron feels like a farewell letter from a master animator confronting the passage of time. It's a dense, challenging, and emotionally resonant work that rewards patient viewers with one of the most profound experiences Studio Ghibli has ever crafted.


2. Howl's Moving Castle (2004)

Hayao Miyazaki’s sweeping fantasy blends anti-war themes with whimsical romance in one of Studio Ghibli’s most visually inventive works.

Review Summary

Howl’s Moving Castle (2004) is a dazzling, imaginative tale that pairs classic fairy-tale elements with sharp political undertones. Adapted loosely from Diana Wynne Jones’s novel, the film follows Sophie, a shy young woman who is transformed into an old woman by a spiteful witch. In search of a cure, she finds refuge in the enchanted, ever-shifting home of the mysterious and flamboyant wizard Howl.

As Sophie becomes entangled in Howl’s world one filled with fire demons, magical contracts, and a devastating war, Miyazaki weaves a rich tapestry of character-driven emotion and anti-war sentiment. The heart of the story lies in Sophie’s quiet strength and personal growth, juxtaposed with Howl’s inner turmoil and his resistance to being consumed by the horrors of conflict.

Visually, the film is a triumph. The titular castle, groaning, walking, and belching steam, is a masterpiece of design, reflecting the film’s eccentric, layered world. Joe Hisaishi’s sweeping score elevates the emotional stakes, lending grandeur to both the tender and the chaotic moments.

While the plot can at times feel nonlinear or dreamlike, Howl’s Moving Castle thrives on mood, metaphor, and emotional resonance. It’s a story about transformation, not just in body, but in courage, love, and perspective. Beneath its whimsy lies a deeply human message: that compassion and self-discovery are the truest forms of magic.


1. Princess Mononoke (1997)

Epic in scale and uncompromising in vision, Hayao Miyazaki’s Princess Mononoke is a landmark of animated cinema. It's an intense, morally complex fable about humanity’s relationship with nature.

Review Summary

Released in 1997, Princess Mononoke marked a turning point for Studio Ghibli: darker, more mature, and thematically ambitious. The story follows Ashitaka, a young warrior cursed by a demon boar, who journeys west in search of a cure. There he finds himself caught in a violent conflict between the industrialized Iron Town, led by the determined Lady Eboshi, and the forest gods and animal spirits fighting to preserve their home, chief among them San, a fierce human girl raised by wolves.

Rather than offering simple heroes and villains, Miyazaki paints a world where every character is driven by believable motives: survival, progress, protection, love. The film doesn’t demonize technology or idealize nature, it presents both as forces in tension, each capable of cruelty and beauty. This moral ambiguity gives Princess Mononoke its power, challenging audiences to think beyond binaries.

The animation is breathtaking, lush forests, ethereal spirits, and visceral battle scenes rendered in exquisite hand-drawn detail. Joe Hisaishi’s haunting score adds to the film’s gravitas, underscoring its spiritual and emotional depth.

Princess Mononoke is not a fairy tale, but a myth, a story that feels ancient and immediate at once. It’s a deeply felt environmental epic, a meditation on violence and harmony, and one of Miyazaki’s most profound achievements.
